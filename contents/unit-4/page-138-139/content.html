2   One hundred flamingos, all running to gather speed for takeoff can mean that more than one toe will get stepped on. And although such long legs can cause accidents, they are also an advantage. They help flamingos to wade along the edges of a lagoon without wetting their feathers. In addition, the membranes, or webs, between flamingos' toes help them to propel themselves through the water or walk on the bottom without sinking into the mud. Flamingos like to rest standing on only one leg.

3   It is not so easy for short-legged birds to poke through the bottom of a marsh. But with their lanky legs, flamingos stir up the bottom, creating a thick, muddy cloud in the water. They bend their long, long necks and poke their heads into the water, processing the sediment as if they were steam shovels dredging a port, only in miniature.

4   With their beaks they take gulps of the settling cloud and filter it to get the food out. How do they do that? Their tongues act like pumps that push the muddy water out through the rims of their beaks, where they have tiny platelets that serve as filters. A disturbance causes this flock of flamingos to fly away in Nakuru Lake, Kenya. They probably won't return for some time.

5   If someone disturbs flamingos while they are eating, they fly away, and it is a long time before they return. A flamingo communicates with other flamingos through the way it calls out and moves. That is why, when one gets scared, the whole flock takes off and escapes together. If they are disturbed too often, they cannot feed properly. They may get sick or even lose their unique color due to the fact that the pink of their feathers comes from the microscopic plants, or algae, and other organisms that they eat. The algae contain a substance called carotene, which is also found in carrots.

6   When flamingos live in zoos, the zookeepers must make sure that their food does not lack carotene so that their color won't fade. Some people say that if flamingos lose their color, they cannot reproduce.

7   Flamingos fly in flocks, with their necks and legs stretched out. They move their wings skillfully while flying or keep them extended to glide. They can reach speeds of up to 48 miles per hour (60 km/h) in the air. During the mating season, future parents have their courtship while flying to the flock's chosen nesting place.
